History of Somalia

Back in the 1800s, Somalia was known for strong clans, rich culture, and busy trade along the coast. Somali traders traveled across the Red Sea and Indian Ocean, doing business with places in the Middle East and Asia. Different sultanates and communities controlled their own areas, and people depended a lot on livestock, trade, and poetry, which was a big part of Somali culture. Toward the end of the century, European countries started coming into Africa during the Scramble for Africa, and Britain and Italy took control of different Somali regions. Even with colonization, many Somalis resisted foreign rule and fought to protect their land, religion, and traditions.

In the 1900s, Somalia went through many important changes. After years of colonial rule, British Somaliland and Italian Somaliland joined together in 1960 to create the independent nation of Somalia. People were proud and hopeful for the future of their new country. Later, in 1969, Siad Barre came to power after a military coup and ruled for many years. At first, some development took place, but problems grew over time, and after the government collapsed in 1991, civil war and instability spread across the country and Somaliland was created. Even through hardship, Somali people stayed strong, holding on to their language, Islam, poetry, and traditions, while Somali communities around the world continued to support their homeland.
